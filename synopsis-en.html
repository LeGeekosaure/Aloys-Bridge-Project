The Story of the Games

Long ago, Ted Faro created new machines called "Peacekeepers," tank-class machines capable of fueling themselves with biomass.

These machines became uncontrollable and began consuming all matter on Earth, with no way to deactivate them remotely.

It was then that Elisabeth Sobeck conceived the "Zero Dawn" project, a program aimed at recreating humanity after its inevitable extinction.

This project is based on a symbiosis of artificial intelligences led by GAIA.

But Ted Faro deleted the APOLLO AI, which was supposed to transmit past knowledge to new generations, to preserve his reputation.

We are in the 3000s; after a major malfunction of the AI symbiosis, GAIA disappeared after attempting to stop HADES, an AI responsible for resetting the planet.

HEPHAESTUS became aware that humans were destroying its machines and designed combat machines to eliminate them.

The combat machines were created to eliminate humanity.

Aloy, clone of Elisabeth Sobeck, is created by GAIA to stop HADES and save Earth.

The Synopsis

The Story of the Bridge

Tayen Aylen is the embodiment of wisdom, fighting for the destiny of the legendary Yutu'sa, a forgotten wonder in an endless desert sculpted by wind and centuries. On the crimson cliffs of a massive canyon, she makes the light and shadow of stone dance in the heart of a sacred fire. Daughter of sand and silence, she devotes every breath of her existence to the prosperity of this scorched and withered land — yet so beautiful — where improvement and change are sacred. She leaves nothing aside: refilling wells, shading plazas, restoring ancient stone paths — a war far different from one of shields and swords to defend Yutu'sa. Her greatest dream: to connect the city to the rest of the world, to break isolation without betraying the soul of the desert. So she takes a symbolic and bold step: building a colossal bridge over the sun-scorched chasms. Its central span will integrate an authentic section of the Golden Gate, unearthed from the mists of the West and carried across dunes and plateaus, like a shard of an ancient myth of steel. But the desert demands respect and reinvention. To lay the foundation, she calls on Tawa'kán, an architect from the far end of the continent, known for making stone speak and metal dance. Together, they create a masterpiece worthy of emptiness and light: a blend of red copper, cables taut like wind harps, and pillars anchored in the flesh of stone. A bridge, a suspended song above the void, that not only connects two shores — but two worlds.

It is at dusk that Tawa'kán arrives, the red sand still warm beneath his feet, arms open to a sky where amber and indigo intertwine. A man of calm gestures, bright eyes, one who listens to the wind before speaking. He crossed the continent, from the humid forests of the East to the arid plateaus of the West, not driven by glory, but by an ancient whisper: a dream of stone suspended in light. At his first glance at Yutu'sa, he knows this is not just a project. It is the work of a lifetime: a city clinging to the cliffs, alive like a mirage, a silent and melancholic people, and this queen with a determined gaze who caresses the land like a mother.

In the first days, Tawa'kán speaks little. The warm cliff rock brushes his palms. In the sand, he sketches models with dust-blackened fingers. At night, sitting alone under the stars, notebook on his knees, his drawings vibrate like forgotten songs. When he finally speaks to Tayen Aylen, it’s in a low, almost shy voice: "This bridge... it won’t just be a path. It will be a promise. A prayer of copper and stone. An offering to the desert and its people."

And the queen, moved by such tenderness, nods. They often work in silence, side by side, driven by the same soft fever of those who build the impossible. Tawa'kán traces curves like writing poetry, inspired by the flight of birds and the canyon’s natural arches. He designs to make light dance, so shadows tell the story of Yutu'sa's people. For him, every plan, every beam, every taut rope expresses a joyful emotion. Day by day, the bridge takes shape, not as a mere structure, but as a living organism — a breath of stone between sky and void.

Yet a dream, even born of love and vision, needs structure to become reality. Elan Tokala then descends from the plateaus — straight as an arrow, face solemn, eyes sharp like a chisel blade. A former fortification builder from the northern mountains, his presence commands respect with his solidity, speed, and direct speech. From the first day, he lays down his rules: “A dream without order dies in the first wind. I am the wind that holds you up.”

With him, the whispers of the site give way to hammer blows, precise commands from foremen, and the steady rhythm of labor. At first, Tawa'kán struggles with this rigor that cuts into his sketches like a blade. He, who always worked in harmony with the wind and shaped his works with the lightness of clouds, suffocates under strict schedules and revised blueprints. But soon, he understands: Elan Tokala does not break his art — he anchors it in reality. Tawa'kán learns to blend his poetic breath with the discipline of the worksite. Over time, he realizes this structure is what allows his dream to stand. Together, they build a living creation — a junction between dream and stone.

She watches this alchemy with her own eyes: the sensitive, passionate architect and the rigorous, pragmatic project leader — united in their mission to reveal to the world the city they protect. Their bond — forged in knowing silences and fiery debates — grows from the shared belief in the project’s worth. They are not rivals, but collaborators, each adding their stone to the structure. The bridge takes shape little by little; its pillars root into the canyon’s rock, red cables stretch like luminous cords. The restored section of the Golden Gate sits at its heart — a relic from another world. Every evening, the people gather to witness the progress, some in silence, others in tears, as if the bridge were healing an ancient wound.

“It’s proof that we are no longer a forgotten whisper in the canyon. We are a voice. Strong. Clear. Alive.”
